The ten rules

  1. Hook first — Tension in 5–30s. No greeting before value.
  2. Title = contract — Deliver the promise in sentence one.
  3. 8–14 min essays — ~158 wpm for style benchmark.
  4. Five beats — Hook → context → body → turn → close.
  5. Bullets on camera — One idea per breath.
  6. AI = speed — Not your POV.
  7. Shorts = best 30s — Hooks work alone.
  8. Farsi + Vancouver — Underserved niche.
  9. Trust before sponsors — Local deals later.
  10. Ship and iterate — Fix retention each video.

Video structure

0:00–0:30 HOOK Surprising fact or place image 0:30–2:00 CONTEXT Why viewer should care NOW 2:00–70% BODY History, data, how-it-works 70–85% TURN Policy vs people Last 60s CLOSE One takeaway + question

Skills by topic

On-camera

  • Open with vocal variety: pitch, pace, pause — not monotone.
  • Speak from the chest for weight; nasal voice loses authority.
  • Cut filler (um, ah) — silence is stronger than noise.
  • Use hand gestures visibly; hidden hands create distrust.
  • One idea per sentence. Pause between ideas.
  • For beginners: bullet points beside the lens, not a full teleprompter script.
  • Get permission in the first 10 seconds — why should they listen?

YouTube success

  • The 5-second rule: when you feel resistance, move in 5 seconds or your brain talks you out of it.
  • Titles and thumbnails are promises — deliver in the first 10 seconds.
  • Retention beats views: fix drop-off at 30s before chasing more uploads.
  • Practice deliberately, not just repetition — one fix per video.
  • Consistency beats perfection for new channels.

Style benchmark

  • Open with a place-based image: one house, one bridge, one skyline change.
  • Scale creates hook: "$2BN project", "thousands of these houses".
  • Body = how it works (engineering, history, zoning) with dry humor as relief.
  • Turn = human cost (businesses blocked, renters priced out).
  • Target 8–14 min, ~150–160 wpm for video essays.
